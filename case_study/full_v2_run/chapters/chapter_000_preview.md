The Descent Begins

The Iron Citadel's war-chamber held maps bleached white as bone. Krath stood at the stone table where someone else's certainty had once plotted supply lines and siege preparations, and felt the weight of abandonment settle around him like armor he had never asked to wear. The dust here was undisturbed—not the fine accumulation of months, but the compressed sediment of years. Decades. The withdrawal had been methodical. No bodies. No fire-damage. No signs of collapse.

Whoever had commanded this place had not fled. They had chosen to leave.

His boots rang against stone floors that remembered the weight of living footsteps, and the sound carried up through corridors carved from rock older than the current age. The masonry spoke of builders who understood that some fortresses are meant to outlast their purpose. In the high windows, light fell through air thick with motes that drifted like prayers in a dead language.

Krath moved through the Citadel's geometry with the precision of a man reading his own future written in stone. Each gatehouse. Each guardroom. Each junction where passages spiraled downward toward the Archive's upper tiers. He had not come here to occupy. He had come here to understand why they stopped trying.

The question followed him down.

---

In the Garden of Echoes, Selunis knelt among statues worn smooth by centuries, their faces erased, their names swallowed by time. She did not dust off her robes when she rose. The dust was part of the prayer now, part of the question she had been carrying without knowing its weight. The sky overhead was the color of old bone, and her footsteps—when she finally took them—seemed to arrive before her feet touched stone.

The Garden's spiral paths curved inward toward thought rather than destination. Each step echoed twice: once in the present, once in some temporal fold that made her aware she was walking both toward and away from the same point. The copper taste on her tongue sharpened as she turned westward, toward the Iron Citadel's distant gates.

Time moved differently here. She could feel it bending around her like cloth, showing her the shape of footsteps she would take before her body decided to take them. Behind her, the statues watched with erased eyes. Ahead, something vast and patient drew breath in a rhythm that matched her pulse.

By the time the Citadel's iron gates rose into view, she was no longer certain how much time had passed. The distinction had stopped mattering. She was walking toward an arrival that felt like remembering.

---

Voss emerged from the Crucible's shadows with the efficient grace of someone who had stopped pretending ceremony mattered years ago. The testing ground behind them hummed with broken machinery—devices that counted to seven and then cut themselves off mid-breath, as if interrupted by something too vast to acknowledge. The copper taste in the air was metallic, electric, almost vocal.

The path to the Iron Citadel revealed itself without maps or guidance. The world seemed eager to show the way. As they walked, Voss could see Selunis perhaps a hundred paces ahead, her robes trailing behind her like shed skin. From somewhere deep within the Citadel's bowels came the echo of boot heels on stone—Krath, moving through abandonment with the certainty of someone who had always known where the passages led.

The great gates, when they materialized from dust and distance, were iron wrought with deliberate elegance. Not crude fortification, but architecture meant to be read before it was opened. Voss paused at the threshold—not from reverence, but to observe. The air here tasted of copper and old decisions. Somewhere in the depths, a dust-devil of disturbed air rose carrying the scent of very old paper.

They understood: some doors are meant to be questions before they become answers.

---

Edra descended from the Archive's central rotunda into bone-halls where lesser questions had already been answered. The orderly catalogues and numbered shelves gave way to raw stone, older stone, stone that remembered when this place was not yet a repository but a first attempt at permanence. Her lamp swung with each step, casting moving shadows on walls scarred with marks that predated writing itself.

The air grew colder as she moved deeper. Not the cold of winter, but the cold of deep earth, of things undisturbed for centuries. The dust beneath her boots was compressed sediment—the accumulation of time itself, settled into layers that spoke of ages when the Archive was something else entirely.

Her lamp-light caught on fragments of clay tablets, inscriptions in scripts that had no name in any modern tongue. But it was the marks on the raw stone that drew her attention: deliberate gouges arranged in patterns of seven, separated by intervals of unmarked rock. Not random scarring. Intentional notation. Someone—or something—had tried to create permanent records before language existed to hold meaning stable.

She did not rush. Rushing would be disrespect to the evidence. Instead, she descended methodically, the only sounds her measured breathing and the creak of the lamp's shutters marking time in a place that had learned to count differently.

---

Maren followed Edra's lamp-light down stone passages where the temperature dropped with each step. Above, the Archive's orderly records spoke of deaths the institution had chosen to acknowledge. But here, in these lower chambers, were marks that spoke of attempts at communication older than the idea that things should be remembered rather than simply endured.

She carried no lamp of her own, so she stayed close to Edra's light and began to write—not in a journal, but in the margins of her own memory, the only record that would matter when they reached whatever waited in the depths. The inscriptions on the walls seemed to pulse with a rhythm that matched her heartbeat: seven marks, unmarked stone, seven again. A pattern that spoke of counting toward something, or counting away from it.

The copper taste in the air grew stronger as they descended. Behind them, the sound of footsteps on stone—Krath's measured descent, moving like water finding inevitable cracks in the Archive's foundations. The walls around them bore witness to something that had tried to speak through stone before the First Sound made speech possible.

Maren wrote in darkness, trusting her fingers to find the right words even when her eyes could not see them. Some truths could only be recorded in the dark.

---

By the time Krath reached the descending passage, the temperature had dropped further still. He moved without haste, each footfall inevitable rather than hurried. The Archive's geometry was already mapped in his mind—he knew where these passages led, knew that the bone-halls extended deeper than any fortress's foundations should reach.

Ahead, Edra's lamp swung like a pendulum marking seconds in a clock that had been running for centuries. Behind her, Maren moved like someone taking dictation from the darkness itself. The air between them compressed as Krath's presence settled into the space, adding weight that made the lamp-light flicker.

He did not announce himself. He simply arrived—a third shadow where there had been two, walking downward with the precision of a man who knew that arriving first was not victory. Arriving whole was.

From deeper still came a sound that might have been stone settling, or might have been something very old drawing breath. The rhythm was patient, deliberate, ancient. Seven beats, then silence. Seven beats, then silence. As if something in the depths was counting toward a moment it had been waiting centuries to reach.

---

Selunis reached the iron gates as the sky overhead deepened from bone-color to ash. Her footsteps had been arriving before she took them for so long that the reversal felt like prayer—a way of moving through time that acknowledged time was moving through her as well. The copper taste sharpened into something almost musical, almost alive.

She did not enter through the gates. Instead, she followed the passages that spiraled downward from the Citadel's foundations—the same routes Krath had taken without hesitation, the same ones Edra had descended methodically, the same ones Maren had followed into lamp-lit darkness. The rhythm of her footsteps was a paradox: one echo before, one echo after, as if she was walking both toward and away from the same unreachable point.

The passages led downward through stone that grew older with each level descended. The walls here bore scars in patterns of seven, deliberate marks that spoke of something trying to communicate before communication had been invented. As she moved deeper, the copper-bright air grew denser, more textured, weighted with attention.

Behind her, somewhere in the Citadel's upper chambers, she could hear Voss's footsteps—arriving exactly when they should, present tense, real. Ahead, the sound of voices echoing through raw stone: Krath, Edra, Maren, gathered in the depths where questions older than language waited to be asked.

---

Voss abandoned the Crucible without ceremony, leaving behind the broken machines that counted to seven and stopped, as if they had been interrupted by something too vast to name. The descent began at a run, then slowed to something more deliberate. Their lamp-light cut through copper-thick air that tasted of electricity and ancient patience.

The passages spiraled downward in a geometry that felt less like architecture and more like the inside of something swallowing. The walls were scarred with deliberate marks—seven-fold patterns separated by unmarked stone, evidence of communication attempts that predated the need for communication itself. Each cluster passed like a heartbeat.

Below, they could hear the others: Krath's weight settling into stone that anticipated his presence, Edra's measured breathing, Maren's pen scratching against something harder than paper. The sound from the depths continued its patient rhythm—not mechanical now, but organic, alive, the pulse of something that had been waiting in the earth's heart for longer than the Citadel had stood.

Voss walked downward with the certainty of someone who had set fire to their own temple and now had to walk into the flames to see what had actually been real.

---

The convergence came without fanfare. Edra's lamp steadied against scarred stone as the others arrived—Krath emerging from shadow like inevitability made flesh, Maren with her hand pressed against marks that pulsed with internal light, Selunis moving with footsteps that echoed in both directions through time, Voss carrying the weight of broken certainties and the copper taste of questions that refused to stay unasked.

They stood in a chamber carved from rock older than the Archive, older than the Citadel, older than the idea that things should be built rather than simply discovered. The walls rose around them like the ribs of something vast and patient. The seven-marks glowed with light that came from within the stone itself—not reflected, not borrowed, but generated by something that had been waiting in the earth's depths for five specific people to arrive and ask the right question in the right way.

The sound from below paused. Not stopped. Paused. As if something ancient had heard its name spoken and was considering whether to answer.

Maren's fingers pressed deeper into the seventh mark, and the stone yielded—not breaking, but opening, like a door that had been waiting centuries for someone to realize it was not locked. The copper-thick air grew denser, more present, weighted with the attention of something vast that had stopped being patient and started being curious.

The five stood in geometry older than choice, breathing air that tasted of questions and time and the moment when the distinction between witness and witnessed finally collapsed into something simpler: presence, recognition, the sound of doors opening from the inside.

Far above, the Iron Citadel's foundations shuddered—not with warning, but with something that might have been relief. In the depths below, something drew breath for the first time in centuries and began, very softly, to speak.
