# Writing Rules

## Tone

The universe is not grimdark. It is not utopian. It is **everything**.

A single universe must hold horror and wonder, tragedy and comedy, intimacy and cosmic scale. The tone of any given story should match its subject — but the setting as a whole should feel like a place where all these things coexist, the way they do in real history.

Write vividly, but with purpose. Avoid empty purple prose. If a sentence is beautiful, it should also be doing work.

## Scale

Stories can range from:
- A god-like entity contemplating the birth of matter
- An empire collapsing over centuries
- Two soldiers talking the night before a battle that will reshape a continent
- A child on an alien world finding something they shouldn't
- A merchant's ledger that accidentally records the last known mention of a vanished species

**The scale does not determine the importance.** A slice-of-life story set during a world-ending event can carry more weight than the event itself.

## Characters

- Cultures are not monoliths. Every faction has internal tensions, dissidents, reformers, loyalists, and people who just want to get through the day.
- Villains believe they are right, or at least necessary.
- Heroes make mistakes that matter.
- Minor characters are still people. Give them a detail that suggests a life beyond their scene.

## Worldbuilding in Stories

- Never stop the story to deliver a lore dump. Reveal the world through what characters notice, assume, argue about, and take for granted.
- The most effective worldbuilding is the thing a character doesn't explain because everyone in their world already knows it.
- If a piece of lore matters to the story, it should be woven into action or dialogue. If it doesn't matter to the story, it doesn't belong in the story (it belongs in a lore file).

## The Observer in Stories

- The Observer should feel like a shadow across ages, not a character who shows up constantly.
- When the Observer appears, it should feel significant — earned.
- Different eras should have different relationships to the Observer: worship, fear, denial, scholarly debate, complete ignorance.
- The Observer's interior experience (when shown) should feel genuinely alien — not a human wearing a cosmic costume.

## Continuity Discipline

- Before writing a major story or lore piece, check what already exists about the involved entities.
- After writing, register new entities, events, and facts in the memory systems.
- If you discover a continuity error while writing, don't ignore it — either fix it or flag it as a truth-layer discrepancy.

## What Stays Unwritten

Some things should be left as gaps, implications, or fragments:
- The Observer's full nature and origin
- What lies outside the universe (if anything)
- Whether the universe has a purpose
- What happens after the final epoch
- Certain ancient events that all records agree happened but none agree on the details

These gaps are features. They give the universe room to breathe and grow.
